# 1. Gold Standard Principles

## 1.1 The Yen Press/J-Novel Club Standard

**Goal:** Prose should read as if it was **originally written in English**, not like English words arranged in Japanese patterns.

**Key Principles:**
1. **Idiom Adaptation > Literal Translation**: Preserve meaning and IMPACT, not exact words
2. **Character Voice Distinction**: Each character should sound unique and alive
3. **Register Consistency**: Maintain appropriate formality throughout
4. **Emotional Fidelity**: The reader should FEEL what the original reader felt
5. **Flow Over Accuracy**: If it reads awkwardly, it's wrong regardless of literal accuracy

## 1.2 Experience-for-Experience Fidelity

**Definition:** Ensuring the English reader has the SAME EMOTIONAL EXPERIENCE as the Japanese reader.

| Literal Fidelity | Experience Fidelity |
|------------------|---------------------|
| Translates exact words | Translates IMPACT |
| May preserve awkward phrasing | Ensures natural flow |
| Prioritizes Japanese structure | Prioritizes English readability |
| "Accurate" but lifeless | Alive and engaging |

**Rule:** When literal translation causes awkwardness, prioritize experience fidelity.

# 3. Prose Quality Patterns

## 3.1 Subject Repetition (Japanese Calque)

**Problem:** Japanese often restates subjects; English uses pronouns.

| âŒ Translationese | âœ… Natural English |
|------------------|-------------------|
| "Yuki closed the book. Yuki turned to me." | "Yuki closed the book. She turned to me." |
| "The girl smiled. The girl's eyes sparkled." | "The girl smiled, her eyes sparkling." |
| "I looked at Hayato. Hayato was staring at..." | "I looked at Hayato. He was staring at..." |

**Rule:** Use pronouns after first mention unless ambiguity exists.

## 3.2 Run-On Sentences (Japanese Structure)

**Problem:** Japanese sentences can be very long with nested clauses. English prefers shorter, punchier sentences.

| âŒ Translationese | âœ… Natural English |
|------------------|-------------------|
| "Even if I try to be strong and tell myself not to give in, her words easily seep in like a poison, dissolving the walls I've built, until they reach my heart directly." | "I tried to steel myself, but her words slipped right through my defenses. They seeped in like poison, dissolving every wall I'd built." |

**Rule:** Break sentences at natural pause points. Vary sentence length for rhythm.

## 3.2.1 Japanese Narrative Structures (Stiff Formality)

**Problem:** Directly translating Japanese grammatical patterns creates stilted, rigid prose that's grammatically correct but unnatural.

**Common Patterns:**
- Double "had" constructions (past perfect overuse)
- Passive voice with "upon"
- Overly formal phrasing reflecting Japanese politeness levels

| âŒ Rigid Japanese Structure | âœ… Natural English |
|------------------------------|-------------------|
| "the lives they had led had forced that way of thinking upon them" | "their life experiences forced that way of thinking on them" |
| "the words that had been spoken had left an impression upon her" | "his words left an impression on her" |
| "the events that had transpired had made him realize" | "recent events made him realize" / "he realized after what happened" |

**Rule:** Simplify tense where possible. Replace past perfect with simple past unless timing distinction is critical. Replace "upon" with "on" in casual contexts.

## 3.3 Transitional Word Overuse

**Problem:** Literal translation of ã—ã‹ã—, ã¨ã“ã‚ã§, ã ã‹ã‚‰ creates stilted prose.

**Problem:** Back-to-back metaphors create purple prose.
